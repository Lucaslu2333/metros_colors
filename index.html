When you travel to a city, have you ever paid attention to the colors of the metro lines? The color of each line is very diverse, but they usually seem very harmonious and unified. You might even notice that many cities have more or less the same color lines. These colors are certainly not randomly selected and assigned to each line. What guidelines did the designers follow when designing them? For different city metro lines, what will be the difference in color selection? In this article, we will analyze it from four perspectives of color choices.

Color for Distinguishable Path [1]

New York City's subway color coding dates back to the mid 60s when the city was in an economic downturn and people were staying off the rundown, haphazardly organized subways. To give the system a fresh, user-friendly look, the Transit Authority turned to graphic design, then an up-and-coming profession. They hired Italian designer Massimo Vignelli and Dutch designer Bob Noorda, both of whom were proponents of the popular “Swiss” style that featured solid, bright colors, simplistic illustrations, typographic grids, and the sans-serif font Helvetica. The men combined these elements into the 364-page New York City Transit Authority Graphics Standards Manual, forming the basis for the subway design we know today. [2]

The Red Line is red because it winds through Harvard, home of the Crimson.
The Green Line is green because it runs along Frederick Law Olmsted’s Emerald Necklace parks.
The Blue Line is blue because it travels under the watery depths of Boston Harbor.
The Orange Line is orange because it runs beneath a part of Washington Street originally called Orange Street. [3]
Color from Landmarks & Terrain
"In an effort to become a more modern transportation system, the MBTA (Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority) opted to brighten up each of the four branches in different shades. Officials didn’t just choose any old colors, though. There’s specific reasoning behind each one." [3]
The color of the first five metro lines in Tokyo was based on the exterior color of the cars. The red and yellow colors come from the cars observed during early investigations of the metro in the UK and Germany,  respectively. The remaining comes from the design of Tokyo's subsequent car colors. [4]
